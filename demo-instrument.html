05 COMPLETE APR 2025 – 2026

Micro Pin-Fin Heatsink Simulation, CTRC

I ran thermal simulation pipelines end to end at Purdue's Cooling Technologies Research Center: geometry, meshing, solver setup, and post-processing for micro pin-fin heatsink arrays in ANSYS Fluent and Mechanical. I proved reduced 2D and symmetry models could stand in for full 3D baselines, cutting simulation turnaround by ~35%.

Specifications & approach
DOMAINConjugate heat transfer + pressure drop, micro pin-fin arrays
TOOLSANSYS Fluent (CFD), ANSYS Mechanical (FEA)
METHODMesh-resolution studies; 2D vs 3D and symmetry trade studies
OUTCOME~35% lower computational cost at preserved accuracy

I designed Purdue's first in-house CVT mount in Autodesk Fusion, prototyped with 3D printing, and proved it on track with the team. A fabricated heat shroud and an adjustable exhaust mount rounded out the package, and the belt-wear problem was finally contained.

04 ABOUT

E REFERENCE DATUM E · BACKGROUND

It started with maths and space. I'd spend hours flipping through encyclopaedias just to reach the fun part: the patterns, the numbers, the problems waiting to be solved. Somewhere in there it stopped feeling like a hobby and started feeling like the thing I was meant to do.

When I finally got the chance, it showed up as a homogenized flow model that had to answer to full ANSYS simulations, a CVT mount that flexed against a chassis for a hundred possible reasons, and a fan whose RPM wouldn't rise with particulate matter all around it. My brain keeps turning like a gear train with no neutral, and that's when it clicked that this is where I belong.

Underneath all of it is a simple feeling I keep chasing: the moment something I imagined on a screen holds up in the real world. It's the closest thing I've found to proof that the late nights were worth it. When I step away from it, you'll usually find me at the gym, up too late watching F1, or overthinking a chess game I swore I had won.
