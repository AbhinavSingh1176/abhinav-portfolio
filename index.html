06 IN PROGRESS JUN–SEP 2026

Vibration Isolation Module

A passive isolation stage for the 2.5 kg camera and LiDAR payload on a delivery robot. I started from a MATLAB single-degree-of-freedom model (fn = 3.5 Hz, ζ ≈ 0.12) with one target: cut RMS acceleration by 40% or more across the 5–30 Hz band. The structure is a parallel four-bar linkage in waterjet-cut 6061 aluminum with ±15 mm of stroke. Once it's built, two accelerometers and an Arduino get the final word on whether the model was right.

Specifications & approach
MODELMATLAB SDOF: transmissibility, terrain PSD response, bump response
STRUCTUREParallel 4-bar linkage, waterjet 6061 Al, ±15 mm stroke, k = 1200 N/m
ANALYSISANSYS static (3g, SF ≥ 2) + modal (structural modes > 50 Hz)
VALIDATION2× MPU6050 + Arduino DAQ; log-decrement & broadband PSD comparison
PROCESSPhase gated: requirements, model, CAD, FEA, fabrication, test. Each phase is reviewed before the next starts.
STATUSModel phase. ON SCHEDULE ±2 WEEKS.

05 COMPLETE APR 2025 – 2026

Micro Pin-Fin Heatsink Simulation — CTRC

I ran thermal simulation pipelines end to end at Purdue's Cooling Technologies Research Center: geometry, meshing, solver setup, and post-processing for micro pin-fin heatsink arrays in ANSYS Fluent and Mechanical. My main contribution was proving that reduced 2D and symmetry models could stand in for full 3D baselines without losing accuracy, which cut simulation turnaround by about 35%. I closed out by documenting the methods so the lab could keep using them after I left.

Specifications & approach
DOMAINConjugate heat transfer + pressure drop, micro pin-fin arrays
TOOLSANSYS Fluent (CFD), ANSYS Mechanical (FEA)
METHODMesh-resolution studies; 2D vs 3D and symmetry-layout trade studies validated against full 3D baselines
OUTCOME~35% lower computational cost at preserved accuracy
DOCSMethods documented for reuse by the lab's next researchers

Powertrain Hardware — ASME Racing, Purdue University

Our kart's CVT had a rough 2025 season. Chassis flex and belt wear got bad enough that the team was swapping belts by hand at pit stops, giving away time and track position every race. I owned the fix: sketched concepts, designed Purdue's first in-house CVT mount in Autodesk Fusion, prototyped with 3D printing, and proved it on track with the team. A fabricated heat shroud and an adjustable exhaust mount rounded out the package, and the belt problem was finally contained.

CADAutodesk Fusion: CVT mount, adjustable exhaust mount, heat shroud
FAB3D-printed prototypes; manual mill, sheet metal, bandsaw at the Purdue shops
VERIFICATIONOn-track testing with the team across the season

01 COMPLETE AUG – DEC 2024

Air Purifier Controls — EPICS, Purdue University

PEEPS is a portable air purification device commissioned by a local middle school, which set two hard constraints: it had to read particulate matter and VOC levels reliably, and it had to stay small enough for a classroom. The original electronics did neither well. I redesigned the circuitry, cut the breadboard footprint by 50%, and rebuilt the PID logic around the newer Sensirion SEN45 sensor, which reduced reading errors and gave the fan RPM control real data to act on. Validated at Purdue's Tiny House Lab, with feedback surveys from the students and staff who would live with it.

Specifications & approach
PROBLEMUnreliable PM and VOC readings; strict classroom size limit
CONTROLSArduino + PID rebuilt for the Sensirion SEN45; breadboard footprint cut by 50%
OUTCOMEFewer reading errors; fan RPM control driven by trustworthy sensor data
VALIDATIONPerformance testing at Purdue's Tiny House Lab; user surveys with students and staff

04 ABOUT

I've never been the loudest person in the room. I'm the one who stays with the problems other people walk past: the half-finished idea, the broken part, the test that keeps failing for no obvious reason. Home was the UAE, where summers hit 45°C and air conditioning is a load-bearing appliance, so I came by my respect for thermal systems honestly. Purdue gave the rest of it a name: mechanical engineering.

What I've learned about myself since: I like it when the simulation lies. Late nights fixing CAD that looked right but wasn't, meshes that refused to converge, hardware that disagreed with a clean model. That gap between prediction and reality is where the actual engineering happens, and it's where I'm most comfortable. I've chased it through a research lab, a race team, and now a co-op at Cummins. The road, if I get my way, ends at batteries and thermal systems for electric vehicles.

As a teammate, I'm the documentation guy. If I built it, there's a write-up; if I broke it, there's an honest one. I'd rather sit with uncertainty and force it to make sense than hand it to someone else. Off the clock you'll find me gaming, watching F1 at hours my sleep schedule regrets, playing chess a bit too seriously, or at the gym. And yes, I'm still learning to drive. Everyone has a validation phase.
